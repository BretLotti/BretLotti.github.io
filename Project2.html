Vinyl Cutting

Vinyl Cutting in Digital Fabrication

First off, I would most definitely consider vinyl cutting to be part of digital fabrication.The process of vinyl cutting involves creating a digital design that is then created physically using the vinyl cutter.From what I understand, that whole process of digital design to physical creation is exactly what digital fabrication is. So it would be odd to aruge vinyl cutting does not fit under the digital fabrication umbrella.

Vector Vs. Raster Images

Raster images are much more common than vector images as they are pixel based and the types of images we see everytime we take a picture. Image file types such as jpgs, pngs, and gifs are all types of raster images. Vector images on the other hand are much less common. Being defined using math and vector software. You will largely see vector images used for things such as engraving and vinyl cutting. Like in this project, where we had a jpg image that we then made into a vector image in order to cut out the design.

Project Intro

The assigment was to create a design for a shirt that was then cut using the vinyl cutter. The design had to be at least 4"x4" and have a related qoute of at least 15 characters.

Design Process

The first step was creating a design uisng Adobe Illustrator. This proved to be one of the hardest steps for me as I could not for the life of me think of a design that I wanted to do. Eventually I settled on the idea of doing a Game of Thrones themed shirt (one of my favorite tv shows). I knew that I wanted to do the wolf crest of House Stark from the show. Which is the head of a wolf. As for my text, I used the title of the ruler of the Stark's hold from the show. I came up with two possible designs I liked. Though I eventually went with the simple "The King In The North".


Cutting and Printing

Now for the really fun part. Printing out, cutting, and applying the image to the shirt. This first required getting the cutting machine ready. Different types of vinyl require different levels of force. For example, the attire vinyl we used for this project required 102g of force.



After the machine cuts the design into a sheet of vinyl. It's time to peel and cut away the extra vinyl that is not part of the image we want on the shirt. This was a fairly slow process because I had pretty styled font and many smaller parts that I did not want to peel off on accident.



Heat Pressing and Trouble

The final step is placing the design on the shirt and pressing it using the heat press. At this point, I had decided the back of my shirt was the best place for my design. This is also where I ran into some trouble. I used the heat press exactly as I was supposed to, but the vinyl had a hard time sticking. Not even a lab worker knew why my vinyl was not pressing correctly(Shout out to John for the help!). After letting the shirt cool and very carefully removing the plastic. My design was pressed onto the shirt. However, some letters were not holding well and I lost two tiny details that can hardly be seen. With that being said, I think it was a rather sucessful first attempt at vinyl cutting and applying a design to a shirt!
